Graph generation models have shown strong performance across various domains. 2 However, most of them assume homophilic graph structures where connected 3 nodes share similar labels. This assumption overlooks heterophilic graphs, which 4 are common in practice and contain edges between nodes with different labels. 5 Motivated by this, we first theoretically analyze how standard message passing 6 in graph generation fails on heterophilic graphs by disproportionately favoring 7 same-label neighbors during aggregation, and provide a theoretical upper bound 8 on the resulting neighbor influence gap. Informed by our theoretical analysis, 9 we propose a graph-aware aggregation mechanism that preserves label-related 10 distinctions while effectively incorporating structural information from different11 label neighbors into the reverse denoising process. Extensive experiments on four 12 real world datasets show that our method consistently outperforms existing graph 13 generation models on both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. 14 1 Introduction  15 Graph-structured data naturally appears in many real-world scenarios [1], ranging from social 16 networks [2] to chemical compounds [3]. Generating realistic graphs has therefore become a key 17 technique to advance a variety of fields, and many methods have been proposed in recent years [4]. 18 Despite their significant success, most existing graph generation models rely on the homophily 19 assumption that edges tend to connect nodes of the same class label [5]. However, heterophilic 20 connections, which link nodes of different classes, also widely exist in real-world graphs [6]. For 21 example, in online transaction networks, fraudulent accounts tend to connect with normal customers 22 rather than with other fraudsters [7]. Similarly, in amino acid interaction networks, amino acids with 23 different properties are more likely to interact with each other [8]. These observations highlight the 24 need to explore graph learning under heterophily.  25 To this end, many efforts have been made to study graph learning under heterophily, but most of 26 them focus on node classification [6]. These methods are designed to improve label prediction on 27 heterophilic graphs while overlooking the graph structure itself. In addition, a small number of works 28 explore link prediction under heterophily, where the goal is to predict missing edges between nodes 29 with different labels. However, link prediction operates on a fixed set of nodes and an existing partial 30 structure, and only requires the model to decide whether a candidate edge exists. In contrast, graph 31 generation needs to produce the entire graph from scratch, including both the graph structure and the 32 node features that support these patterns. This makes heterophilic link prediction methods unable to 33 address the full graph generation problem, since they neither generate node features nor model the 34 joint distribution of structure and features under heterophily.  35 Despite its importance for many real-world applications, the problem of heterophilic graph generation 36 remains largely unexplored. Developing a generation framework for heterophilic graphs is therefore 37 both timely and necessary. To achieve this, several challenges need to be addressed: i) Joint 38 Generation of Heterophilic Structure and Node Features. Unlike node classification or link prediction, which operate on a fixed graph, graph generation needs to produce both the edges and 40 the node features from scratch. Heterophilic graphs make this harder, as connections do not follow 41 feature similarity. A model that learns to generate features and edges separately tends to produce a 42 graph where the structure no longer matches the features, so that heterophilic edges either disappear 43 or connect random nodes. Generating heterophilic graphs therefore requires a generation framework 44 that jointly learns the dependency between features and structure throughout the entire generation 45 process. ii) Entanglement of Label-related and Structural Information. Node representations 46 on heterophilic graphs need to serve two distinct purposes during generation, i.e., preserving label47 discriminative information that distinguishes nodes with different labels, and capturing heterophilic 48 structural information. Existing message passing mixes these two types of information into a single 49 representation, so that aggregation along heterophilic edges compromises both the label identity 50 and the graph structural information. Hence, how to disentangle these two roles within a unified 51 representation framework remains an open problem. iii) Lack of Theoretical Understanding of 52 Message Passing on Heterophilic Generation. Most existing graph generation methods empirically 53 adapt their models to heterophilic graphs, without formally analyzing how the underlying model 54 behaves under heterophily and how this behavior propagates to the generation of heterophilic edges. 55 Establishing this analysis is difficult due to two compounding sources of uncertainty. The first is 56 the layer-wise representation gap introduced when same-label and different-label neighbors are 57 aggregated under a shared smoothing rule. The second is the way this gap is carried through the 58 reverse denoising process and ultimately reshapes the reconstruction quality of heterophilic edges.  59 To tackle the above challenges, we propose Heterophilic Graph Generation (HeteroGen), a diffusion60 based generation framework specifically designed for heterophilic graphs. To the best of our knowl61 edge, this is the first work to provide a theoretical analysis of how the underlying model distorts node 62 representations in heterophilic graph generation and to quantify how this distortion propagates to 63 the reconstruction of heterophilic edges, offering formal guidance for designing generation models 64 on heterophilic graphs. Specifically, we first develop a graph-aware message passing mechanism that 65 disentangles label-related and label-irrelevant information and aggregates same-label and different66 label neighbors through different paths, supported by theory-guided regularization that preserves 67 label distinctions and heterophilic structural information during generation. In addition, we provide 68 a theoretical analysis that quantifies how existing aggregation affects node representations in het69 erophilic graph generation, and derive an upper bound that quantifies how this distortion propagates 70 to the reconstruction of heterophilic edges. To summarize, our contributions are threefold:  71 • To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first work to tackle the graph generation task 72 beyond the homophilic assumption.  73 • We propose HeteroGen, a heterophily-aware generation framework that disentangles label74 related and label-irrelevant information during message passing, and provide a theoretical 75 analysis that derives an upper bound on the neighbor influence gap to quantify how existing 76 aggregation affects heterophilic graph generation.  77 • We conduct extensive experiments on four real-world graphs, showing that the proposed 78 method exhibits superior performance over existing graph generation baselines in terms of 79 both graph generation task and downstream task.
2 Related Works  81 Graph Generation Models. Graph generation models have recently shown great promise for graph 82 generation tasks, and they can be broadly divided into one-shot methods and autoregressive [9]. 83 Specifically, one-shot methods generate the whole graph in a single forward pass [10]. These methods 84 are often built on variational autoencoders [11], generative adversarial networks [12], and diffusion85 based models [13], and usually have advantages such as node permutation invariance and a more 86 global representation of graph structures. However, since they generate many graph components 87 jointly, they may struggle to model fine-grained dependencies among nodes and edges and to preserve 88 structural validity. In contrast, autoregressive methods generate graphs step by step by adding 89 nodes, edges, or substructures in a sequential order. These methods are often built on recurrent 90 neural networks [14] and reinforcement learning [15]. This sequential design offers finer control 91 over graph construction. Despite the progress made by both paradigms, these methods still rely 92 on homophily-biased representation learning and edge modeling, i.e., they encode graph structure 93 through neighborhood aggregation to obtain similarity-based node representations, and then generate
synthetic graphs from these smoothed representations. However, in heterophilic graphs, neighboring 95 nodes often carry very different information, so local aggregation may mix incompatible signals, 96 weaken discriminative node representations, and bias the generator toward homophilic connections, 97 thereby degrading the quality of generated synthetic graphs. Hence, there is a need to explore graph 98 generation methods for heterophilic graphs.  99 Heterophilic Graph Neural Networks. Heterophilic graph neural networks have attracted increas100 ing attention in recent years [16] and there is a rich literature on graph neural networks addressing 101 heterophily [17, 18, 19]. Existing methods can be divided into two groups, i.e., neighbor extension 102 methods and adaptive message aggregation methods [5]. The former aim to expand the receptive 103 field to capture informative distant nodes [20], while the latter design adaptive aggregation schemes 104 to distinguish useful heterophilic and homophilic signals [21]. For instance, MixHop [17] aggregates 105 messages from neighbors at different hops and combines them to capture long-range informative de106 pendencies, while FAGCN [22] employs a self-gating mechanism to adaptively balance low-frequency 107 and high-frequency signals from neighbors. However, those methods are mainly developed for classi108 fication task, and do not explicitly model the generative process of graph structures. Therefore, they 109 cannot be directly applied to graph generation in heterophilic settings.  110 Different from the above works, this paper proposes a heterophilic graph generation model that 111 addresses both the homophily bias in representation learning and the lack of heterophilic modeling in 112 graph generation, with its design informed by theoretical analysis on how same-label and different113 label neighbors jointly affect edge reconstruction. In addition, the proposed graph-aware message 114 passing mechanism and heterophily-aware regularizer is flexible, allowing it to be applied in both 115 link prediction models and generative models.

Finally, the proposed model and its training objectives are presented. 132 4.1 Heterophily-Aware Regularization  133 Existing diffusion-based graph generation models usually parameterize the reverse denoising process 134 with a GNN backbone. Specifically, a diffusion-based graph generation model defines a forward 135 Markov transition kernel q(xt | xt−1) to gradually corrupt the observed graph data into a simple noise 136 distribution, and then learns a reverse denoising transition pθ(xt−1 | xt) to recover the graph structure. 137 Given that the reverse process relies on node representations learned by message passing, its gener138 ation behavior is closely tied to the underlying neighborhood aggregation mechanism. Essentially, 139 existing message passing methods in GNNs update node representations by aggregating information 140 from local neighbors. This aggregation acts as a smoothing operation that makes connected nodes 141 toward similar representations. On homophilic graphs, neighboring nodes typically share the same 142 label, so smoothing reinforces label-consistent information and creates node representations well 143 aligned with the downstream task. However, in heterophilic graphs, neighboring nodes often possess 144 different labels, and message passing should preserve these distinctions rather than smooth them out.
However, in heterophilic graphs, neighboring nodes often possess different labels, and aggregation 146 node representations should capture these distinctions rather than smooth them out. Consequently, 147 the same smoothing operation diminishes important differences among neighbors, resulting in node 148 representations that lose label-related information and degrade performance on heterophilic graphs.  149 To this end, we analyze how existing message passing aggregates node representations on heterophilic 150 graphs. Since a standard message passing layer can be viewed as a one-step gradient update of a 151 smoothness-based optimization problem on graph representations [23], it encourages connected nodes 152 to move toward similar representations by reducing their representation difference. On homophilic 153 graphs, this reduction is effective because connected nodes mainly share similar labels. On het154 erophilic graphs, however, connected nodes mainly have different labels and should not be smoothed 155 into similar representations. Specifically, smoothing toward different-label neighbors pushes a node’s 156 representation away from its own label identity, while the structural information carried by these 157 neighbors is not properly captured because it is mixed with conflicting label information. These two 158 effects suggest that node representations contain label-related information and label-irrelevant struc159 tural information that should be treated differently during aggregation. To this end, we disentangle 160 the node representation a label-related node representation that preserves the label identity of the 161 node and a label-irrelevant node representation that captures structural information from neighbors. 162 Under this decomposition, we find that standard message passing disproportionately aligns node 163 representations with same-label neighbors while under-utilizing the structural information carried 164 by different-label neighbors, which biases the generator toward homophilic patterns. To evaluate the effectiveness of HeteroGen on graph generation, 330 we compare it with state-of-the-art graph generation baselines on Cora and Film, and report the results 331 in Table 1. Additional results on Citeseer and Squirrel are provided in the Appendix. We observe 332 that HeteroGen outperforms all baseline methods across most evaluation metrics. On the homophilic 333 dataset Cora, HeteroGen achieves competitive performance with the baselines across all five metrics. 334 This is because HeteroGen’s graph-aware message passing preserves smooth structural information 335 through the same-label aggregation path, so it does not sacrifice generation quality on homophilic 336 graphs. On the heterophilic dataset Film, HeteroGen substantially outperforms all baselines across 337 all metrics, with large improvements on the homophily-preservation metrics NHD and EHD. The 338 reason is that existing methods rely on a smoothing-based aggregation that collapses different-label 339 neighbors into similar representations, which causes the generator to produce graphs that drift toward 340 homophilic patterns regardless of the original structure. HeteroGen disentangles label-related and 341 label-irrelevant information and processes same-label and different-label neighbors through separate 342 paths, which directly addresses this limitation and accurately preserves heterophilic structures.
